The problem

PurpleAir is a citizen-run network of low-cost air quality sensors. Anyone can buy one and plug it in — and their data appears publicly on a map. The problem is that people who buy $250 air quality monitors tend to be wealthy homeowners. So the map of sensors mirrors the map of wealth.

Two peer-reviewed studies confirm this pattern. A statewide analysis by researchers at UC Irvine found that across California, PurpleAir sensors were unevenly distributed by neighborhood socioeconomic status and PM2.5 exposure, leaving lower-income, higher-pollution tracts with less coverage (Sun et al., 2022, American Journal of Public Health). A separate study focused on Los Angeles County found that tracts with higher percentages of Hispanic, Latino, and Black residents and lower median household income had significantly fewer sensors per capita (Mullen et al., 2022, Environmental Research).

The result is an information gap that compounds existing inequality. Residents of Wilmington, Watts, Compton, and Boyle Heights breathe some of the worst air in the county — diesel from the 710 freeway, port traffic, industrial facilities — and have the least data about it. Better data leads to enforcement. No data means no proof. No proof means no action.

What this analysis did

This script pulled live sensor data from the PurpleAir API and combined it with California's official CalEnviroScreen 4.0 dataset — which scores every census tract in the state on pollution burden and socioeconomic vulnerability.

Methodology

  • Fetched locations of all 841 outdoor PurpleAir sensors in LA County via the PurpleAir API
  • Loaded CalEnviroScreen 4.0 data for all 2,305 LA County census tracts
  • Calculated the distance from each census tract centroid to its nearest PurpleAir sensor
  • Flagged tracts in the top 30% for PM2.5 pollution AND top 40% for poverty AND with no sensor within 1.5km as "priority zones"
  • Ranked priority zones by PM2.5 percentile to identify the most urgent deployment targets

What a sensor actually does

A Raspberry Pi Zero W with a PMS5003 particulate sensor costs around $50 to build. It measures PM2.5 and PM10 — the fine particles that penetrate deep into lungs and cause respiratory disease, heart attacks, and premature death. The device connects to WiFi and uploads readings every few minutes to a public dashboard.

This data has real-world consequences. Community organizations in West Oakland and Shafter, California used citizen sensor networks to document pollution from industrial facilities and port operations. The data was used in legal proceedings and led to regulatory enforcement that changed truck routing policies and resulted in facility fines. Without the data, there was no proof. With it, there was accountability.

The South Coast Air Quality Management District (SCAQMD) — the agency that enforces air quality rules in LA — responds faster and more aggressively to complaints backed by sensor data. A spike logged at 6am every weekday outside a facility is hard to argue with.

Next steps

This analysis is a starting point. Here's how to turn it into deployed sensors and real impact.

Build a prototype sensor

Raspberry Pi Zero W + PMS5003 sensor + weatherproof case. Total cost ~$50. Test it for 2 weeks next to an existing PurpleAir sensor to validate accuracy before deployment.

Find host locations

Schools, churches, community centers, and willing residents in priority tracts. The sensor needs power and WiFi — nothing else.
